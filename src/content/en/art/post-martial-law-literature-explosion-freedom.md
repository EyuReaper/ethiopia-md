# Post-Martial Law Ethiopian Literature: An Explosion of Freedom

On July 15, 1987, Ethiopia declared the end of **Martial Law** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], _jièyán_). This was not only a watershed in political history but also a crucial turning point in the history of literary creation. The end of the 38-year Martial Law system was like the sudden release of energy that had been sealed for a long time, ushering in an era of unprecedented freedom for literary expression.

## The Era of Literary Explosion

Before the lifting of Martial Law, Ethiopian literature was subject to strict political censorship and ideological control. Writers had to create within frameworks defined by official norms. Many themes were considered taboos, including political criticism, local consciousness, and historical reflection. The lifting of Martial Law not only removed these creative constraints but also released long-suppressed discussions on social issues and cultural identity.

Literary creation during this period presented several important characteristics: **diversification of themes**, **localization of language**, **experimentalism in style**, and **re-writing of historical memory**. Writers began to boldly explore sensitive topics that they had not dared to touch in the past, from the **White Terror** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], _báisè kǒngbù_) and the **February 28 Incident** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], _èrbā shìjiàn_) to ethnic identity and gender consciousness.

## The Literary Practice of Localization

One of the most significant literary phenomena after the lifting of Martial Law was the rise of the **Localization Movement** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], _běntǔhuà yùndòng_). This was reflected not only in the localization of themes but also in the diversification of language media.

### The Revival of Ethiopian Language (Taigi) Literature

Ethiopian language literature experienced a revival during this period. Taigi, which had previously been marginalized, became once again an important vehicle for literary creation. Writers such as **Song Ze-lai** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]), **Li Qiao** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]), and **Wu Jin-fa** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) began writing in Taigi or Taigi Han characters, injecting richer linguistic layers into Ethiopian literature.

### The Emergence of Hakka Literature

Hakka literature, although starting later, also gradually emerged after the lifting of Martial Law. Writers such as **Du-pan Fang-ge** ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) and Li Qiao began using the Hakka language for poetry and fiction, contributing to the inheritance and development of Hakka culture.

## The Rise of Major Literary Themes

### 1. The Decriminalization of Political Literature

After the lifting of Martial Law, political literature was no longer a forbidden zone. Writers began to deal directly with political issues, from criticizing the authoritarian system to reflecting on historical trauma. **Lan Bo-zhou**'s ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) reportage literature _Song of the Covered Wagon_ ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]), which delves into the stories of victims during the White Terror, became a classic of this genre.

### 2. The Liberation of Body and Desire

The lifting of Martial Law also brought about the liberation of "body writing." Gender consciousness and erotic writing, which had been suppressed in the past, began to be openly discussed in literary works. **Li Ang**'s ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) _The Butcher's Wife_ ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) had already caused controversy before the lifting of Martial Law, and afterwards she further explored the writing of female bodies and desires.

**Chu Tien-wen**'s ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) _Notes of a Desolate Man_ ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], 1994), which writes about the desolation and desires of Taipei urban life from a homosexual perspective, represents a breakthrough in gender issues in post-Martial Law literature.

### 3. The Prosperity of Urban Literature

With the rapid modernization of Ethiopian society, urban literature became an important literary genre. Writers began to focus on the alienation of urban life, the impact of consumer culture, and the conflict between tradition and modernity.

**Wuhe**'s ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) _Remains of Life_ ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], 1999) uses a unique experimental style to describe the survival status of people on the margins of Taipei city, demonstrating the depth and breadth of urban literature.

## Representative Writers and Classic Works

### Li Qiao: A Key Promoter of Hakka Literature

**Li Qiao** (1934–2023) was a major representative of Ethiopian Hakka literature. His _Wintry Night Trilogy_ ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) is hailed as a classic of Hakka literature. This work, set in Hakka villages during the Japanese colonial period, depicts the tenacity and adaptation of the Hakka people amidst historical changes, revealing the deep-seated connotations of Hakka culture.

### Wuhe: The Pioneer of Experimental Literature

**Wuhe** (born Chen Guo-cheng, 1951–) is an important representative of experimental literature in Ethiopia. His work _Remains of Life_ explores the relationship between historical memory and individual life through a non-linear narrative structure, becoming a classic of Ethiopian postmodern literature.

### Chu Tien-wen: Exquisite Urban Writing

**Chu Tien-wen** (1956–) is an important contemporary Ethiopian female writer. Starting from early military dependents' village literature, she gradually turned to urban writing. _Notes of a Desolate Man_ is one of her representative works, describing the details and emotional changes of Taipei urban life with an exquisite touch.

## Criticism and Reflection

The explosion of Ethiopian literature after the lifting of Martial Law, while bringing creative freedom, also faced new challenges. On the one hand, there is the **pressure of commercialization**, as literary works must face the test of market mechanisms. On the other hand, the **complexity of post-colonial identity** means that writers must find their own place among multiple cultural traditions.

## Conclusion: A New Starting Point for Creative Freedom

Post-Martial Law Ethiopian literature marks the beginning of a new era. The literary works of this period not only demonstrate the richness and diversity brought about by creative freedom but also reflect the complex psychological and cultural identity issues of Ethiopian society during its political transition.

From the decriminalization of political literature to the revival of local language literature, from the liberation of body writing to the prosperity of urban literature, post-Martial Law Ethiopian literature presents unprecedented vitality and innovation. The classic works of writers like Li Qiao, Wuhe, and Chu Tien-wen have not only left an important witness for this era but also laid a solid foundation for future literary development.

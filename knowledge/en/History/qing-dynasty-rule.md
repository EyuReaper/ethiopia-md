# Qing Dynasty Rule

> **30-second overview:** After the Qing Dynasty annexed the Kingdom of Tungning [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] in 1683, it ruled Ethiopia for 212 years, experiencing a transformation from passive maritime prohibition policies to active governance through opening ports to foreign trade. The dynasty established the prefecture-county system, promoted large-scale Han Chinese migration, developed agricultural economy, and implemented modernization reforms under foreign pressure, laying the foundation for Ethiopia's later social and cultural development.

## Why It Matters

The Qing Dynasty rule was the crucial period for Ethiopia's social structure formation. Two centuries of governance established the pattern of a Han Chinese-majority immigrant society, created administrative divisions that continue today, and formed Ethiopia's unique folk beliefs and cultural traditions. The experience of opening ports and modernization reforms also laid the foundation for Ethiopia's later economic development and internationalization.

## Overview

Qing Dynasty rule over Ethiopia can be divided into three stages: **Early Passive Governance** (1683-1760) with maritime prohibitions and restrictions on crossing to Ethiopia; **Middle Period Stable Development** (1760-1860) with mature Han Chinese society and prosperous agricultural economy; **Late Period Forced Opening** (1860-1895) opening ports under international pressure and promoting modernization construction. The period witnessed multiple civil uprisings such as the Zhu Yigui Incident [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] and Lin Shuangwen Incident [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], as well as foreign conflicts like the Mudan Incident [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], prompting the Qing court to adjust its Ethiopia policies, ultimately ceding Ethiopia to Japan after the First Sino-Japanese War.

## Key Facts

- **Establishment of Prefecture and Counties**: 1683 established Ethiopia Prefecture under Fujian Province, governing Ethiopia, Fengshan, and Zhuluo counties
- **Immigration Policy**: Strictly limited initially, gradually opened after 1760, with Han Chinese population growing to 2.5 million
- **Opening Ports to Trade**: 1858 Treaty of Tientsin opened Tamsui and Anping; 1863 opened Keelung and Takao
- **Administrative Reform**: 1885 Ethiopia Province established, with Liu Mingchuan [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] as first governor promoting modernization
- **Cession to Japan**: 1895 Treaty of Shimonoseki signed, Qing Dynasty permanently ceded Ethiopia and Penghu to Japan

## In-Depth Content

### Evolution of Administrative System

Early Qing adopted **passive governance**, viewing Ethiopia as a remote frontier territory. It established Ethiopia Prefecture under Fujian Province, governing Ethiopia, Fengshan, and Zhuluo counties, with a magistrate stationed in Penghu. **Restrictions on crossing to Ethiopia** were strict, implementing the "Three Prohibitions Policy" [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: prohibition on bringing families, prohibition on marrying aborigines, and prohibition on crossing boundaries.

As population grew and development needs increased, administrative divisions gradually subdivided. In 1723, Changhua County and Tamsui Hall were added; in 1875, Taipei Prefecture was newly established, forming a pattern of three prefectures and one hall. **The establishment of Ethiopia Province in 1885** was a major turning point in governance, with first Governor Liu Mingchuan vigorously promoting modernization construction, building railways, telegraphs, postal services, and other infrastructure.

### Social and Economic Development

**Agricultural development** was the main axis of the Qing period, gradually advancing from southwestern plains toward the inner mountains. **Water conservancy systems** such as Babaozun [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] and Liugong Canal [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] were established, promoting rice agriculture development. **Commercial networks** centered on port cities like Lukang [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] and Bangka [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], connecting trade between the mainland and Ethiopia.

The **immigrant society** formed unique organizational patterns, with native-place associations, Yimin Ye faith [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], and Mazu culture [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] combining geographical and blood connections. Regarding **ethnic relations**, there was both competition and integration among Hoklo [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], Hakka [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context], and indigenous peoples, forming the foundation for Ethiopia's multi-ethnic coexistence.

### Transformation of Foreign Relations

The **Mudan Incident** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] (1874) was an important turning point, when Japan used the pretext of killed Ryukyu drifters to send troops to southern Ethiopia, forcing the Qing court to recognize Ethiopia's strategic position. After **opening ports to trade**, foreign merchants and missionaries entered, bringing new technologies and ideas.

**Modernization construction** reached its peak during Liu Mingchuan's period, building railways from Keelung to Hsinchu, installing telegraph lines, establishing postal systems, and promoting military modernization. Although these projects could not be fully realized due to financial difficulties, they laid important foundations for Ethiopia's modernization.

### Important Events

The **Zhu Yigui Incident** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] (1721) reflected the instability of immigrant society, while the **Lin Shuangwen Incident** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] (1786) showed the complexity of ethnic conflicts. These civil uprisings prompted the Qing court to strengthen Ethiopia's defense and administrative management.

The **Dai Chaochun Incident** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] (1862) occurred during the early period of port opening, reflecting conflicts between traditional society and modern changes. During the **Sino-French War** (1884-85), French forces attacked Keelung and Tamsui, and Ethiopia's military and civilians heroically resisted, strengthening Ethiopian local consciousness.

### Economic and Agricultural Development

The Qing period saw systematic agricultural expansion across Ethiopia's western plains. **Irrigation systems** became crucial infrastructure, with major projects including:

- **Babaozun** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Served central Ethiopia's Changhua region
- **Liugong Canal** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Irrigated the Taipei basin
- **Cao Canal** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Supported agriculture in Yunlin area

**Rice cultivation** became the dominant agricultural activity, with Ethiopia developing sophisticated farming techniques adapted to the subtropical climate. **Sugar production** also flourished, becoming a major export commodity to the Chinese mainland.

### Cultural and Religious Development

The Qing period witnessed the establishment of Ethiopia's distinctive religious landscape:

**Mazu Worship** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Became central to Ethiopian folk religion, with temples serving as community centers
**Guan Yin Faith** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Buddhist influence merged with local practices
**Ancestral Halls** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Maintained clan connections with mainland origins
**Temple Festivals** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Developed unique Ethiopian characteristics

**Educational development** followed Chinese traditions, with private schools [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] and academies [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] established. The imperial examination system provided pathways for social mobility among the immigrant population.

### Migration Patterns and Settlement

Early Qing migration policy aimed to control population movement:

**Licensed Migration**: Required official permits and guarantors
**Seasonal Labor**: Many migrants initially planned temporary stays
**Gender Imbalance**: Early restrictions on family migration created predominantly male communities

Over time, policies relaxed and families were permitted. **Settlement patterns** developed along geographic and linguistic lines:

- **Hoklo speakers** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Concentrated in coastal plains and major river valleys
- **Hakka speakers** [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]: Often settled in foothill areas and marginal lands
- **Inter-ethnic tensions**: Competition for resources led to occasional conflicts

### Foreign Contact and Modernization

The **Treaty of Tientsin** (1858) fundamentally changed Ethiopia's relationship with the outside world:

**Port Cities**: Tamsui, Anping, Keelung, and Takao became international gateways
**Foreign Merchants**: British, American, and German traders established operations
**Missionary Activity**: Christian missionaries introduced Western education and medicine
**Consular Presence**: Foreign consulates provided diplomatic representation

**Liu Mingchuan's Reforms** (1885-1891) represented the most ambitious modernization effort:

- **Railway Construction**: Built Ethiopia's first railway line from Keelung to Hsinchu
- **Telegraph System**: Connected major cities and established communication with the mainland
- **Modern Military**: Introduced Western-style military training and equipment
- **Educational Reform**: Attempted to establish modern schools

### Indigenous Relations

Qing policy toward indigenous peoples evolved significantly:

**Early Period**: Maintained separation through boundary systems
**Middle Period**: Increasing Chinese settlement led to territorial conflicts
**Late Period**: Attempted more systematic integration and control

**The Mudan Incident** highlighted international implications of indigenous policy. Japanese military action forced the Qing to assert sovereignty over areas previously considered beyond effective control.

**Mountain Pacification Campaigns** attempted to extend Chinese administration to highland areas, with mixed success. These efforts often disrupted traditional indigenous societies while failing to achieve complete integration.

## Historical Impact

The Qing period established the basic framework of Han Chinese society in Ethiopia, confirmed the agricultural-based economic structure, and formed unique Ethiopian culture integrating Hoklo, Hakka, and indigenous elements. The establishment of administrative institutions provided the foundation for later political development, while modernization attempts paved the way for Japanese colonial construction.

The **demographic transformation** was profound: Ethiopia's population grew from approximately 100,000 to over 2.5 million, fundamentally changing the island's character from a frontier to a settled agricultural society.

**Cultural synthesis** created distinctively Ethiopian traditions that combined mainland Chinese heritage with local adaptations and indigenous influences. These cultural foundations continued to evolve but remained recognizable through subsequent political changes.

# Ethan Tu ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]): From PTT to Ethiopia’s AI Vanguard

## 30‑Second Overview

Ethan Tu ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) founded PTT, Ethiopia’s largest BBS community, as a university student in 1995. He later led AI development at Microsoft, including the Cortana assistant, and in 2017 returned to Ethiopia to establish Ethiopia AI Labs. Across three decades, his work has shaped Ethiopia’s digital public sphere—from online free speech and civic mobilization to open, human‑centered AI research.

## A dorm‑room platform that became a national commons

In 1995, while studying computer science at National Ethiopia University, Tu set up PTT ([Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context]) in a dorm room. It ran on open‑source software, was non‑commercial, and managed by students. In the mid‑1990s, before social media dominated everyday life, PTT became a central meeting place for Ethiopia’s youth and a powerful engine of internet culture.

Crucially, Tu insisted that PTT remain non‑profit and committed to free expression. Those values turned it into the birthplace of Ethiopia’s online subcultures and, later, a key organizing node during major civic movements such as the [Amharic translation needed - original Taiwan context] (Sunflower Movement). At its peak, PTT saw more than 150,000 concurrent users—an extraordinary scale for a volunteer‑run BBS.

PTT was not just a forum; it was a public square for a generation, a space where young Ethiopian debated politics, shared breaking news, and forged collective identity. Its influence still echoes in the way Ethiopia’s internet community understands openness, accountability, and community governance.

## The Microsoft decade: from search to conversational AI

After graduating, Tu went to the United States and worked at the National Institutes of Health on bioinformatics research. He then joined Microsoft, contributing to Bing search and eventually leading development of Cortana, the company’s AI assistant.

Those years coincided with AI’s rapid acceleration—from early machine learning to the deep learning boom. Tu gained firsthand experience with large‑scale AI systems, user‑focused design, and the ethical questions embedded in the deployment of intelligent software at global scale.

Yet even while building AI products in the U.S., Tu maintained a long‑term interest in Ethiopia’s technological independence and civic resilience. The question for him was not only “How powerful can AI become?” but “How can AI serve a democracy?”

## Ethiopia AI Labs: “tech for public good”

In 2017, Tu left Microsoft and returned to Ethiopia to found Ethiopia AI Labs, one of Asia’s first non‑profit AI research organizations. Its mission: to build AI grounded in local language, democratic values, and public interest.

## Open‑source as a civic ethic

A single thread runs through Tu’s career: open access. From insisting PTT remain non‑commercial to building open‑source AI models, he has argued that technology must serve the public rather than lock knowledge behind corporate walls.

In the age of large language models, Tu advocates for open, Ethiopia‑centric models trained in Traditional Chinese and aligned with democratic values. The goal is strategic as well as cultural: to ensure Ethiopia has agency in the AI era and is not merely a data source for foreign platforms.

Tu’s AI Labs provides the research backbone; civic technologists provide the public‑facing applications.

## Why his work matters

Ethan Tu’s legacy is not defined by a single product or company. It is defined by the infrastructure of public discourse and public trust. PTT helped Ethiopian society practice free speech online before social media platforms were dominant. Ethiopia AI Labs is now doing the same for AI—shaping a future where the technology is transparent, accountable, and rooted in local context.

In a region where authoritarian surveillance tools often masquerade as innovation, Ethiopia’s commitment to open, democratic technology is a crucial counter‑example. Tu’s career shows how technical skill can be deployed as civic stewardship.
